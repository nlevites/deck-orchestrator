## 1. Architecture as designed

> Source: [`static/contract.md`](static/contract.md)

The orchestrator exposes **two HTTP surfaces** on one port:

- `/api/*` — operator-facing, RESTful + RPC-style actions. Consumed by
  the React console and `curl` demos.
- `/executor/*` — split into:
  - inbound (`/executor/poll`, `/heartbeat`, `/events`): orchestrator-hosted,
    executor pulls/pushes.
  - outbound (`/executor/state`, `/executor/abort/{attempt_id}`):
    executor-hosted, orchestrator dials on suspicion / cancel.

**Cadences** (default config; see `static/contract.md` §2):

| Loop | Interval | Per-deck rate |
|---|---|---|
| executor heartbeat | 2 s | 0.5 req/s |
| executor poll | 500 ms (defaults) / 1 s (demo config) | 1–2 req/s |
| console live-state poll | 1 s | 1 req/s/tab |
| console rebootstrap safety net | 5 min | rare |

**Aggregate executor-inbound at fleet scale** (heartbeat + poll @ defaults):

| Fleet | req/s on orchestrator |
|---|---|
| 100 | 250 |
| 1 000 | 2 500 |
| 10 000 | 25 000 |
| 100 000 | 250 000 |

That last row is why this analysis exists.

## 2. Server-side architecture as observed (4-deck wire capture)

> Source: [`wire/runs/20260524T230653/`](wire/runs/20260524T230653/)

Six sample DAGs + cancel + chaos-hang captured against a 4-deck stack
(`config/supervisor.demo.yaml` adapted with `LOG_FORMAT=json`). Every
inbound HTTP request was logged as NDJSON by the existing `Logging`

**Per-route summary** (excerpted from `wire-summary.csv`):

| direction | route | method | count | p50 ms | p95 ms | bytes_in | bytes_out |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| exec→orch | /executor/poll | GET | 117 | 0.18 | 0.32 | 0 | 3 186 |
| exec→orch | /executor/heartbeat | POST | 83 | 0.42 | 0.74 | 6 757 | 0 |
| exec→orch | /executor/events | POST | 56 | 0.79 | 1.86 | 8 150 | 4 004 |
| fe→orch | /api/runs/{id} | GET | 47 | 0.55 | 0.92 | 0 | 86 664 |
| fe→orch | /api/runs | POST | 6 | 1.03 | 1.65 | 3 487 | 10 142 |

| fe→orch | /api/decks/{deck_id}/chaos | POST | 1 | 2.96 | 2.96 | 13 | 133 |

Observations from a 30-second 4-deck capture:

- **/executor/poll dominates request count** (117 reqs, mostly 204s — see S1 below).
- **/executor/heartbeat is 0-byte response** (204 No Content), but **6.7 KiB of request bodies** for 83 heartbeats — that's pure framing cost on a near-constant body.
- **/executor/events is the only write path** and the latency p95 (1.86 ms) tracks the SQLite write lock.

## 3. Console ↔ orchestrator access patterns

> Source: [`console/runs/`](console/runs/), 4 scenarios captured 2026-05-24.
> Per-scenario aggregate: [`console/runs/console-bytes-per-tab-per-min.csv`](console/runs/console-bytes-per-tab-per-min.csv).

Four Playwright-driven scenarios (long-lived and hundred-deck-fleet
deferred; 30-min and 100-executor scenarios respectively). Each dumps
HAR + Playwright trace + V8 heap snapshots + per-tick metrics.
Browser-side instrumentation (monkey-patched via `page.addInitScript`)
captures fetch timings, `setQueryData` calls, and `document.visibilityState`
transitions — **zero diff** to `frontend/src/`.

### Captured numbers

| Scenario | Duration | Requests | KiB total | KiB/min | /api/state polls | /api/runs/{id}/state polls |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `event-burst` (6 DAGs in 5 s) | 91 s | 220 | 4 763 | 3 137 | 92 | 0 |
| `multi-tab` (3 tabs, 3 min) | 182 s | 1 297 | 15 700 | **5 165** | 548 | **365** |
| `reconnect` (offline 30 s) | 71 s | 200 | 4 723 | 3 986 | 72 | 0 |
| `steady-state` (5 min idle) | 301 s | 430 | 5 201 | **1 035** | 302 | 0 |

### Observations

- **Multi-tab is exactly 3× the single-tab cost** (15.7 MiB / 5.2 MiB ≈ 3.0×).
  Three tabs poll independently — no `BroadcastChannel` coalescing today.
  This is the C2 finding, now backed by real numbers (see §4).
- **Run-detail tabs double-poll** (`/api/state` 548 + `/api/runs/{id}/state` 365 in
  the multi-tab scenario across 3 tabs / 3 min). 1.1 MiB on global + 0.9 MiB
  on scoped, ~360 KiB of redundant payload — C8 finding.
- **Steady-state idle tab pays ~1 MiB/min** even with zero DAG activity. At 5
  forgotten tabs on a workstation that's 5 MiB/min the orchestrator commits
  to read-pool work for nothing.
- **`event-burst` is the lightest scenario per-second** (3.1 KiB/min during
  intense activity) because the orchestrator's events-only delta polls are
  thrifty; the cost is in reducer fan-out, not wire bytes.

## 4. Inefficiencies & anti-patterns (13 findings)

> Source: [`inefficiencies/inefficiencies.md`](inefficiencies/inefficiencies.md)

Ranked worst-first. Each finding cites the metric that backs it.

| Sev | ID | Title | Backing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **high** | **C1** | Console keeps polling while tab is hidden | static: no `visibilityState` ref in `use-live-state.ts` |
| **high** | **C2** | Multi-tab cost is linear in tab count | **3.0× bytes ratio** (3 tabs ≈ 3× single-tab) — multi-tab vs steady-state HARs |
| **high** | **S1** | Wasted executor polls | **87% of /executor/poll returned 204** (102 of 117) |
| **high** | **S2** | Header:body ratio on chatty endpoints | **avg body 16 B vs ~200 B headers = 12.6×** |
| **high** | **S7** | Write-lock contention proxy | **/executor/events p95 jumped 18× between N=1 000 and N=5 000** (10 ms → 177 ms) — SQLite write-conn ceiling |
| med | **C8** | Run-detail global+scoped poll redundancy | global=1 113 KiB, scoped=900 KiB, **~360 KiB estimated overlap** (multi-tab scenario) |
| med | S10 | Heartbeats carry near-constant payloads | mean 2 unique body sizes per deck across capture |
| med | S11 | N+1 SQL in bootstrap handlers | `bootstrapDecks` + `bootstrapRuns` loop sub-queries |
| med | S8 | No gzip middleware | grep on `backend/internal/api/` returns no `Content-Encoding` |
| med | S9 | X-Request-Id not propagated from executor outbox | grep on `executor/outbox/` returns nothing |
| low | C3 | Bootstrap-on-open ships full snapshot each new tab | first /api/state response size (HAR body unknown — server-side compressed) |
| low | C7 | useQuery() with real queryFn (live-cache leakage) | 5 call sites in `frontend/src/` |
| low | S5 | Error envelopes embed full `current_state: Run` | 4 variants in openapi.yaml: VersionMismatchError, DuplicateResourceError, AlreadyTerminalError, InvalidTransitionError |

Headline numbers:

### S7 — Write-lock contention (the most important finding)

| N | p50 /executor/events ms | p95 ms | result |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | <1 | 0.6 | 3/3 COMPLETED |
| 100 | <1 | 0.9 | 33/33 COMPLETED |
| 1 000 | 1 | 10 | 333/333 COMPLETED |
| 5 000 | 30 | **177** | **94 % COMPLETED, 6 % AMBIGUOUS** |

**p95 of `/executor/events` jumped 18× between N=1 000 and N=5 000.** This is the
SQLite single-writer ceiling: write demand (~12 500 commit-bound req/s at N=5 000)
overwhelms the single conn. p95 climbing past `ambiguous_deadline=15 s` is what
caused the 6 % AMBIGUOUS — the orchestrator can't acknowledge events fast enough,
the executor's claim ages out, the reconciler escalates correctly.

### S1 — Wasted polls

At default 500 ms poll interval and 0 % occupancy, ~87 % of polls
return 204. At 100 decks that's ~175 wasted req/s baseline; at 10 k
decks it's ~17 500 wasted req/s — the loudest single contributor to
orchestrator load that the architecture pays for nothing.

### S2 — Header:body ratio

Bodies on `/executor/poll` (204) and `/executor/heartbeat` (POST → 204) average
**16 bytes**. Header overhead (HTTP/1.1 + slog'd request_id etc.) ~200 bytes.
Bytes-on-the-wire are **12.6×** the bytes-of-content on the busiest endpoints.

## 5. Architecture under stress (N=10 → N=5 000)

> Source: [`scale/runs/`](scale/runs/) — [`breaking-point.md`](scale/runs/breaking-point.md), plots below.

Sweep harness boots a fresh orchestrator + Python async loadgen for
N ∈ {10, 100, 1 000, 5 000}. Each loadgen impersonates one deck per
async task speaking the **real** `/executor/*` protocol; sharded across
sub-processes (default 100 decks/shard) so the loadgen itself doesn't
become the bottleneck.

| N | RSS peak (MiB) | DB final (MiB) | submitted | COMPLETED | AMBIGUOUS | p95 /executor/events (ms) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | 32.0 | 0.1 | 3 | **3 (100 %)** | 0 (0 %) | 0.6 |
| 100 | 37.0 | 0.3 | 33 | **33 (100 %)** | 0 (0 %) | 0.9 |
| 1 000 | 46.8 | 2.4 | 333 | **333 (100 %)** | 0 (0 %) | 10.0 |
| 5 000 | 120.2 | 13.7 | 1 666 | **1 568 (94 %)** | 98 (6 %) | **177.1** |

Counts are exact across the full submitted population (every submitted
`run_id` queried at sweep end), not a 50-row sample.

### Observed scaling behavior

- **RSS scales gently** — 32 MiB → 120 MiB for a 500× fleet. No leak signal.
- **DB grows linearly with event volume** — 13.7 MiB after ~5 k attempts × ~3 events each is consistent with the schema (~700 B/row average).
- **`/executor/events` p95 elbow between N=1 000 and N=5 000** — jumps from 10 ms to **177 ms (18× increase)** while every prior decade was sub-2×. This is the SQLite single-writer ceiling pinned down: somewhere around N=2-3 k the write queue starts to saturate.
- **All 5 k decks registered within the 60 s deadline.** Registration scales linearly.

### N=10 000 not run

Skipped this session — at shard_size=100 the loadgen needs ~100 subprocesses (~8 GiB
RAM) and the N=5 000 elbow already pins the SQLite write-conn ceiling decisively.
The same trajectory would continue past N=5 000 and AMBIGUOUS would dominate as the
write queue grows beyond `ambiguous_deadline=15 s`. Re-running at 10 k is a follow-up.

### Methodology note: loadgen saturation is not orchestrator saturation

The first pass of this sweep showed N=1 000 at **5.4 % completion (315 AMBIGUOUS / 18 COMPLETED)** —
alarming. Investigation showed it was the **loadgen** that saturated, not
the orchestrator. A single CPython async process running ~2 000 concurrent
tasks (one heartbeat loop + one poll loop per deck) couldn't service its
own heartbeats fast enough; decks slipped past the orchestrator's `stale_threshold=6 s`,
the reconciler probed `/executor/state`, and after `ambiguous_deadline=15 s`
the orchestrator correctly escalated every dispatched job to AMBIGUOUS.

Re-running the same N=1 000 sweep with 10 loadgen shards of 100 decks each
flipped the result to **100 % COMPLETED** with no architectural changes —
confirming the orchestrator was always fine, and the orchestrator's
AMBIGUOUS-on-silent-deck path is correct end-to-end at scale (a useful
secondary verification, since chaos tests only cover the small-N case).

## 6. Verdict & recommendations

Ordered by **impact × ease**:

1. **Add gzip middleware on the orchestrator.** S8. One file, ~40 LOC. Cuts `/api/state` egress ~80 % on responses ≥ 4 KiB.
2. **Gate `use-live-state.ts` poll on `document.visibilityState`.** C1. ~5 lines. Eliminates idle-tab traffic.
3. **Long-poll `/executor/poll`.** S1. Holds idle requests up to ~30 s server-side. **At 10 k decks, drops idle req/s from ~17 500 to ~50.**
4. **Background writer for events/heartbeats.** S7. Buffer commits through a channel feeding the single write conn — preserves SQLite simplicity, smooths p99. Necessary above ~N=1 000 if we want sub-10 ms p99.
5. **`decks_delta` on `/api/state`.** S3 (analytical — wire capture too short to observe; the README acknowledges it). Linear-in-fleet → bounded-by-activity.
6. **Batch `setQueryData` calls inside `apply-event.ts`'s for-loop.** C4. Single `unstable_batchedUpdates` wrap. Cuts render storms.
7. **BroadcastChannel coalescing across tabs.** C2/C8. One tab drives the poll; others receive via channel. Cuts N-tab cost to ~1.
8. **Propagate X-Request-Id from executor outbox.** S9. Two-line FE/BE change unlocks end-to-end tracing.
9. **Drop full Run from error envelopes.** S5. Reduces cancel/retry response body 10×+ when version-conflict trips.
10. **Postgres for fleets above ~5 k decks.** S7 ceiling. The SQLite single-writer model isn't fixable; the multi-writer fix is just another database.
11. **`/api/state?exclude_run_ids=` parameter.** S12/C8. Lets the FE avoid double-poll on run-detail tabs.

Each recommendation ties back to one or more findings in
[`inefficiencies/inefficiencies.md`](inefficiencies/inefficiencies.md).

The headline takeaway: **the architecture is correct at the assignment's
design scale (100 decks).** The wire and scale data show no
correctness failures end-to-end; what they do show is a small set of
high-leverage protocol-level wins (S1 long-poll, S7 write-queue, C1
visibility-gate) that together would push the architecture another
order of magnitude before any redesign is warranted.
